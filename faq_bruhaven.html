Welcome to the official FAQ and behind-the-scenes of your narrative game, Loch Ness Monster: Expedition. Below you'll find answers to the most frequently asked and deeply considered questions about the story, mythology, characters, and creative inspirations.

Why is the Queen described with blue eyes and brown hair?

In Edinburgh, over 57% of people have blue eyes and brown is the most common hair color. That combination struck me visually when I first arrived. It became a symbol of local beauty and identity. The Queen represents the everyday elegance I saw in real Scottish people.

Why is the King emotionally soft and gentle?

I wanted to show a version of masculinity that is noble, protective, but not aggressive. He leads with love and loyalty. He's a warrior when needed, but he's primarily a human being who feels, supports, and listens.

Why a dog named Haggis?

Haggis is inspired by Greyfriars Bobby, the famous loyal Skye Terrier from Edinburgh. I wanted to turn a cultural icon into an emotional anchor. He's named 'Haggis' for warmth and charm â€” something unmistakably Scottish.

Why does Haggis become a Highland Bull?

It symbolizes Scottish strength. Highland cows are iconic here. I had never heard of a Highland bull, so I thought creating one with fire horns would make it feel even more fantastical.

What's in the bag and why?

The bag contains Iron Brew, shortbread, her cloak, her journal, candles, and a stock-and-horn. It represents Scotland through the eyes of a foreigner. Each item symbolises comfort, culture, memory, or survival.

Why do dwarves and fog offer different paths?

The dwarves represent wisdom, community, and rest. The fog represents grief, loneliness, and emotional reflection. Players are tested to choose comfort vs. solitude.

Why a granny dwarf with riddles?

She adds warmth, familiarity, and Scottish folklore charm. Her riddle also has meaning later, so she isn't just comic relief.

Why do selkies appear as the Queen?

Selkies are Scottish mythological creatures. I wanted to introduce local folklore and show how grief makes us believe what we want to see. It's an emotional test.

Why do puzzles appear?

The Queen is a scholar. She leaves behind stories and riddles. Solving puzzles is about proving you understand and respect her mind, not just loving her presence.

Who are the Blue Men of the Minch?

A lesser-known Scottish sea myth. They test the King's intellect with rhyme and challenge, as a metaphor for facing foreign places with humility.

Why the unicorn?

The unicorn is Scotlandâ€™s national animal. It's strange and magical. It doesn't speak or fight; it guides. It rewards the player for patience and trust.

Why is the glowing map a trap?

It was given in a dream, vague and hypnotic. It's a trap for players who rely on tools instead of instinct or emotion. The wrong path leads to grief and loss.

Why include her voice as a trap?

It challenges emotional impulse. Not everything that sounds like love is safe. The King must trust what he's learned, not just what he hears.

Why end at Loch Ness?

It's the most famous myth tied to Scotland. The monster is real but not evil. It symbolizes nature, fate, and myth itself.

Why not kill the monster?

Players get a choice. Mercy is the noble path. The Queen offers a non-violent solution. It emphasizes emotional maturity and the value of compassion.

Why does the Queen use a book to end it?

She's a scholar. It's about knowledge, heritage, and ancient language. The monster dissolves into mist not death, just transformation.

Why no throne room, no celebration?

Because peace is the real ending. No crown, no gold. Just going home. Just being together.
